# Key Sources

## Direct DIF and AI Anchor

Maeda and Lu (2025) is the closest direct precedent. They fine-tuned
Transformer-based LLMs to predict DIF from item text in 42,180 assessment items
and used explainable AI to identify words associated with DIF. This means the
novelty cannot simply be "LLM predicts DIF from text"; a MAPS paper needs a more
specific contribution such as longitudinal psychometric validation, prompt
robustness, bilingual item wording, or mechanism-constrained hypothesis
generation.

- Maeda, H., & Lu, Y. (2025). Finding Words Associated with DIF: Predicting
  Differential Item Functioning Using LLMs and Explainable AI. Journal of
  Educational Measurement, 62(4), 883-906.
  https://doi.org/10.1111/jedm.70017
- Source page: https://experts.umn.edu/en/publications/finding-words-associated-with-dif-predicting-differential-item-fu/

## LLMs in Psychometrics and Assessment

Brickman, Gupta, and Oltmanns (2025) provide a broad psychological-assessment
overview and emphasize validation, bias, privacy, and generalizability as core
design constraints.

- https://doi.org/10.1177/25152459251343582

Hommel et al. (2022) show that transformer-based language models can support
construct-specific automatic item generation, but their role is best treated as
decision support for item authors rather than replacement for psychometric
validation.

- https://doi.org/10.1007/s11336-021-09823-9

Rios (2026) argues that LLMs may improve item-writing efficiency but do not
replace SME review, revision, and psychometric evaluation.

- https://doi.org/10.1080/08957347.2026.2655640

## DIF, Content Review, and Fairness Framing

Suk and Han (2024) extend DIF logic to algorithmic fairness through
"differential algorithmic functioning," which is useful for connecting
psychometric measurement bias to AI fairness.

- https://doi.org/10.3102/10769986231171711

Kraus, Wild, and Hilbert (2024) combine interpretable machine learning with
psychometric modeling to investigate test unfairness, giving a nearby precedent
for ML-assisted interpretation of psychometric bias.

- https://doi.org/10.1177/01466216241238744

Pohl, Schulze, and Stets (2021) emphasize that partial invariance and anchor
selection require substantive item-content judgment, not statistics alone.

- https://doi.org/10.1177/01466216211042809

## LLM Cautions

Palmer, Smith, and Spirling (2024) argue that proprietary language models in
academic research require explicit justification because closed models create
reproducibility and auditability problems.

- https://www.nature.com/articles/s43588-023-00585-1

Grundy et al. (2024) recommend transparency, journal-policy compliance, privacy
protection, and avoiding overreliance when using LLMs in science.

- https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1011767

Farquhar et al. (2024) show hallucination/confabulation remains a central issue
and propose semantic entropy as one detection approach.

- https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-07421-0

Bender et al. (2021) remain a foundational caution that fluent LLM outputs should
not be mistaken for grounded understanding.

- https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445922
